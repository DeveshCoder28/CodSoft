Pandit Bhimsen Gururaj Joshi (1922 â€“ 2011) was a renowned Indian classical vocalist and one of the foremost exponents of the Hindustani classical music tradition. He hailed from the Kirana gharana, a school of North Indian classical music known for its emphasis on the aesthetics of the raga and intricate ornamentation.

Early Life and Training: Born on February 4, 1922, in Gadag, Karnataka, India, Bhimsen Joshi displayed an early interest in music. He received his initial training in Hindustani classical music from his father, a schoolteacher who was also a musician. Later, he became a disciple of Sawai Gandharva, a renowned vocalist and guru, and continued his rigorous training under his guidance.

Vocal Style: Bhimsen Joshi was known for his powerful and resonant voice, which allowed him to effortlessly traverse a wide range of octaves. His renditions of ragas were marked by his ability to evoke deep emotions through his music. He was particularly adept at presenting khayal, a predominant form of classical vocal music that involves intricate improvisation within the framework of a raga.

Contribution to Music: Bhimsen Joshi's contribution to the Indian classical music landscape was immense. He not only preserved the traditional musical heritage of his gharana but also infused his performances with his own unique style. He was known for his soul-stirring renditions of devotional and semi-classical compositions as well.

Awards and Recognitions: Throughout his career, Pandit Bhimsen Joshi received numerous awards and accolades. Some of the most notable awards include the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian award, and the Sangeet Natak Akademi Award. His musical prowess earned him international acclaim as well.

whose original name was Rambhau Kundgolkar from the Kirana Gharana was the Guru of Pandit Bhimsen Joshi. Abdul Karim Khan was a strict disciplinarian - made a contract with his disciples for at least 8 years. His lessons started early hours in the morning followed by rigorous training in the afternoon and evening. Ustad ji did not believe in teaching many Ragas. He taught Rambhau only three Ragas in 8 years: Todi, the morning Raga; Multaani, the midday Raga and Puriyaa, the evening Raga. He firmly believed that proper understanding of these three would open the doors to many more.
